About Grocery Store Access in King County


Created by Emily Siangkam


Grocery Store Access in King County offers a visualization of the relationships between grocery facilities throughout King County and a person's ability to access them. Oftentimes, a person's accessibility for healthy food is limited by whether or not they have access to transportation, or certain types of stores may be located in more affluent areas. This project aims to demonstrate barriers that may prevent people from accessing the resources they need.

Through this project, users can explore the relationship between median household income and grocery store locations, or whether an area has a great amount of people of color. considering the oppression and injustices that Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC) have experienced, These may contribute to the lack of grocery stores present in certain areas. Additionally, users also examine the relationship between public transit routes and grocery stores, to visualize a person's ability to access a grocery store if they were to take the bus. However, it is important to consider the time, distance, and mode of travel that people may need to know to better understand these relationships. Unfortunately, under the time of this project was developed and the availability of data from the King County GIS Open Data site, this information could not be determined at this time.

This project does not encompass every existing grocery store in King County. Although this information was taken from the King County GIS Open Data site, there were only about 200 entries for grocery stores, and was not reflective of every facility located in this region. As such, the data was cleaned to grocery stores that would typically carry fresh produce, meat, dairy products, and other food items a person may shop for. Convenience stores and pharmacies were also excluded, and additional searches using Phantombuster, a data extraction site, was used with search terms of popular stores in the area, such as "Fred Meyer", "Safeway", and "grocery". Obviously, this is not an exhaustive list of those present, but this produced an additional number of stores needed for the purposes of this project.

Inspiration for this project came from the Healthy Food Availability and Food Bank Network Report put out in February 2019. However, considering there is very limited data on what the researchers define as "healthy" food, and other information such as travel time to these locations, further work is needed to fully understand the implications this visualization may have.

Data Attributions

Most of the data for this project came from King County GIS Open Data. From this site, the data for grocery stores median household income, percent people of color, transit routes, and total population in King County. Some of the grocery stores were also located using Google searches with PhantomBuster, a site that allows users to conduct data extractions from web searches.
